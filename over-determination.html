The prune-order decision procedure: check rule-types cheapest-to-prune first. Step 0 = rotation (popcount-preserved). Then gate families in order of flexibility (smaller reachable set = killed more easily = check first). For each: the cheap reach pre-filter says DEAD (skip, no search) or ALIVE; if alive, the exact #operand-combos that fit. You stop at the first family that resolves the rule; the flexible GATE3 residual (CH/F2/OR_XNOR/PAR3) is only reached if everything simpler is dead.

NAND/NOR/XNOR~(a&b) / ~(a|b) / ~(a^b)

CH(a&b)|(~a&c) — a selects b vs c
PAR3a^b^c — parity
OR_XNORh | XNOR(f,g) — OR-term is the 3rd operand
F2 (GATED_XNOR_NAND)(~c & XNOR(a,b)) | (c & NAND(a,b)) — c selects XNOR vs NAND. Symmetric in a,b. The hardest to crack by hand.
AO / AX(f&g)|h / (f&g)^h

Streams: x, ~x, and for k=1..7: shl/shr/rol/ror and their inverts. shl/shr zero-fill (lossy at the edge); rol/ror wrap (permutations). Convention: index 0 = leftmost bit.

Headline: 83.7% of all gate×operand combos are lossy. Only XOR/XNOR/PAR3 (linear) and pure rotations are commonly bijective. F2 is 1-dominant (5/8 of its truth table) which is why sparse inputs explode to dense outputs.
