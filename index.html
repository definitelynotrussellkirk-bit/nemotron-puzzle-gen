Understanding the BIT puzzle as GATE(f, g, h)

Don't learn our solution — learn BIT, or generate it.

We set out to teach the model to search these puzzles — enumerate candidate rules, test, prune. One thing that helped was finding a clean way to show and generate the hardest type, BIT, as a whole-byte gate over three streams. This page is that: the representation, a live calculator, how the data is generated, and what the data looks like.

Honest scope: a data-generation piece, shared as work-in-progress — a way to show and generate BIT. Not a claim we taught the model to solve gates, and not a leaderboard result. The fuller story of what we learned about search is in insights.html.

Why an interactive viewer? We hope it helps people visualize the bit problems — and, paired with the over-determination explorer, watch the search space collapse as each example rules out hypotheses. Many across past competitions have noted how valuable it is to use LLMs to build quick visualizations of your data; this is us doing exactly that, and hoping it's useful to others.

0 · What the BIT puzzle actually is — a full walk-through

If you finished the competition and still didn't quite get the bit puzzle, you're in good company — it seems most people didn't, and (as far as we could tell) the model didn't really either. So here's the whole thing from scratch, assuming no prior familiarity. If you don't care about our solution but want to understand BIT, this section is the point of the repo.

The puzzle

You're shown several examples of an 8-bit input turning into an 8-bit output, all made by one hidden rule. Then you get a new input and must produce its output:

01010001 -> 11011101 00001001 -> 01101101 00010101 -> 01010101 11111111 -> 10000001 10011101 -> 01000101 ... (a few more) ... 00110100 -> ? <- find this

That's the whole task. (This is a real puzzle; the answer is 10010111, and you'll see exactly why below.)

The per-bit view (why it looks hard)

The natural first move is to treat each of the 8 output bits as its own little puzzle — "output bit 3 is some function of the input bits." But a single boolean function of 8 input bits is one of 2256 possibilities, and ~9 examples barely dent that. Seen per-bit, the puzzle feels almost impossible — and that's exactly where most people (and, it seemed, the model) get stuck.

The key idea: it's ONE whole-byte operation

The hidden rule is almost never 8 unrelated bit-functions. It's a single operation on the whole byte: take a few cheap whole-byte views of the input and combine them with one boolean gate:

output = GATE( f(x), g(x), h(x) )

where x is the input byte and f, g, h are cheap transforms of it. This turns the puzzle into a small search instead of a per-bit one.

Streams — the f, g, h

A stream is the input run through one cheap whole-byte op:

  • x — the input unchanged (identity); ~x — every bit flipped (complement).
  • shl k / shr k — shift all bits k places left/right, filling the empty end with 0 (bits that fall off the edge are lost).
  • rol k / ror k — rotate all bits k places, wrapping the bits that fall off back around to the other end (nothing is lost).

On 10110010: shl1 = 01100100 (everything slides left, a 0 enters on the right); rol1 = 01100101 (same slide, but the bit that fell off the left wraps to the right). There are 58 named streams — but only 44 distinct, because for an 8-bit byte rol k and ror (8−k) are literally the same function.

Gates — the combine step

A gate combines the streams bit-by-bit. The simplest are 2-input: AND, OR, XOR, applied at each position of two streams. There are also 3-input families (e.g. OR_XNOR, MAJ3 = majority-of-3, CH = choose) that mix three streams. Each output bit is just the gate applied to the matching bits of the streams — nothing more. The live calculator in §3 below lets you pick three streams and a gate and watch the output computed one bit at a time — the fastest way to feel how it works.

What the whole-byte view buys you

Reading the byte whole collapses the hypothesis space from "2256 per bit" down to 89,086 distinct whole-byte functions. That small class reproduces 100% of the real competition BIT puzzles — every gold answer is some gate over three streams. So the right mental model isn't eight little puzzles; it's "guess one GATE(f,g,h)."

Two questions: the function vs the answer

"Solving BIT" can mean two different things:

  • Search for the FUNCTION — recover the exact hidden rule.
  • Search for the ANSWER — just produce the output for the query input.

Computed on the real puzzles (median 9 examples each), within the whole-byte class:

QuantityValue
exact rule uniquely determined (function search)~58%
all consistent rules agree on the query answer~83%
a random consistent rule, applied to the query~93%
the simplest consistent rule (Occam on arity), applied to the query~99%
random over all functions (ignore the examples)0.39%

Why the simplest consistent rule matches the answer

The examples do not uniquely determine the rule ~42% of the time. The generator builds each puzzle from a low-arity gate over small shifts, so the simplest rule consistent with the examples is usually the one it used. Picking that rule and applying it to the query matches the gold answer ~99% of the time. Reproducible: analysis/occam_solver.py --stats.

The solver, in one sentence

Among all GATE(f,g,h) rules consistent with the examples, take the simplest and apply it to the query. That's the "Autotracer" (analysis/occam_solver.py --demo): on the puzzle up top it returns out = XOR(shl1, ~shl7) and 00110100 → 10010111. (On the real competition BIT rows our internal solver scored ~99.3%, measured on our machine; train.csv isn't shipped here, so that figure isn't reproducible from this repo. The ~99% on random examples is — occam_solver.py --stats.)

Note: the model also did not seem to handle BIT well, which is part of why we spent so long on it. This section stands on its own — you can take the GATE(f,g,h) framing, the numbers, or the solver and ignore the rest of the repo.

2 · The GATE(f, g, h) structure

The per-bit view asks "what produces output bit o[k]?" eight separate times. The whole-byte view asks one question: the output byte is a boolean gate applied across three transformed views (streams) of the input byte x.

output = GATE( f(x), g(x), h(x) )

Each stream is the input byte run through one cheap whole-byte operation. The catalog:

StreamOperationCount
x, ~xidentity and bitwise complement2
shl1..7 / shr1..7logical left / right shift by k (zero-fill)14
rol1..7 / ror1..7rotate left / right by k (wrap-around)14
~shl/~shr/~rol/~rorcomplement of each of the above28

Alias caveat we hit: for an 8-bit byte rol_k == ror_(8−k), so the 58 named streams are only 44 distinct functions. The model literally cannot tell rol1 from ror7 — they compute the same thing — so any grader that demands the exact name wrongly fails a correct equivalent. In analysis we treat two streams as the same when they share a 256-entry truth table — scoring the function, not the label. Generation itself samples uniformly from the 58 names, so aliases get a label-weighted prior (an aliased rotation is drawn ~1.32× as often); dedup happens only in the analysis, not in the sampler.

In our internal solver runs this whole-byte family recovered ~124 of the 137 rows our per-bit decomposition missed (an internal measurement on our own solvers, not reproducible from this public repo alone).

4 · How a puzzle is generated (the forward direction)

Solving is the inverse problem. Generation is trivial and deterministic — which is exactly why we can build an exact oracle:

So a generator is just: (1) pick a gate + a stream triple, (2) pick N random input bytes, (3) emit each x → GATE(f(x),g(x),h(x)) pair, (4) hold one out as the query. The rule that produced it is known, so the answer — and a fully derivable reasoning trace — is known too. The solver runs this in reverse: enumerate the 44 streams × gate families, keep the (gate, f, g, h) that reproduces every example, then apply it to the query.

A derivability lint guards the traces: every line must be a mechanical consequence of the one before it (no naming a stream without showing the bits that reveal it). That lint doubled as a process-reward signal for the GRPO experiments.

5 · What the bit data actually looks like

Two distributions matter. First, the per-bit function mix — our internal measurement over the 12,816 output bits in the 1,602 competition rows we had during the contest (aggregate counts from our own analysis, not a public file) — this is why exotic 3-input families are a trap to over-weight:

Read it plainly: 54% of bits are just copy-or-constant (COPY 39.9 + CONST 14.1), ~99% are 0/1/2-input (the bars above sum to 98.9%), and only ~1% genuinely need a 3-input gate. This matters for generation: sampling gate families uniformly badly misrepresents the real data (it floods you with exotic 3-input gates that barely occur). Our generator weights families to the empirical mix, so the synthetic data looks like the competition data.

Why bit is solvable: a search for ANSWERS, not FUNCTIONS

89,086distinct whole-byte functions — this class reproduces 100% of the real BIT puzzles
~58%of the time the exact RULE is uniquely pinned (search for the function)
~83%of the time the ANSWER is forced — all consistent rules agree (search for the answer)
~99%answer via OCCAM — pick the simplest consistent rule (reproducible on random examples)

Two framings. Search for the function (recover the exact rule): the rule is uniquely determined ~58% of the time. Search for the answer (predict the query output): the answer is logically forced ~83% of the time, a random consistent rule matches it ~93%, and the simplest consistent rule (Occam on arity — lowest gate-arity + simplest streams) matches it ~99%. Random over all functions (ignoring the examples) is 0.39%. We spent most of our time on function search; answer search is what a frequency/simplicity prior — like Tong's per-column method — uses. (Computed on the real puzzles, reproducible on random examples: analysis/occam_solver.py --stats and the --demo "Autotracer".)

One structural fact: 2,266 / 65,536 conceivable single (x→y) pairs are unreachable by any gate in our enumerated whole-byte family — an unreachable pair falsifies the single-shared-gate model (it doesn't rule out arbitrary whole-byte functions); in our corpus those were per-output-bit rows. And the 58 stream names cover only 44 distinct functions — for answering the query only the function matters, never the label.

6 · The CoT formats were search traces — approaches & papers

We came to see most of the custom CoT formats as search traces: enumerate candidate rules, test against the examples, prune, lock. We read the search-supervision literature and borrowed its training shapes. We hoped one thing would be our edge: our solver is an exact, free verifier of every intermediate state, so the weak/learned-verifier bottleneck the literature describes wouldn't apply to us. It didn't translate into a solving win for us — see §7.

Our initial framing — recursive bisection. The instinct we started from: to get from a state A to the goal Z, if the model can't do it in one shot, introduce an intermediate waypoint N, solve A→N and N→Z separately, and keep bisecting until every hop is trivially solvable. That divide-and-conquer / sub-goal instinct is what sent us to the literature; the shapes below are its trainable form (enumerate → verify → prune, with the solver checking each hop).

  • Teach-long, explicit enumerate→test→prune SFT — every step a solver-checkable state→action→observation→prune transition; rejected branches shown in-trace (contrastive CoT for free).
  • STaR / expert-iteration trim — inspired by Zelikman et al., STaR (2022) and Lehnert et al., Searchformer / "Beyond A*" (2024). Those papers filter sampled rationales / learn A* search dynamics and bootstrap shorter traces; the "keep the shortest verified trace under a declining length budget, else the canonical trace" rule is our adaptation, not their method.
  • GRPO process / progress reward — GRPO is from Shao et al., DeepSeekMath (2024, arXiv:2402.03300); the Hamming-distance and "gold still reachable" rewards are our proposed shaping, not from that paper.
  • Step-level process supervision (Lightman et al., Let's Verify Step by Step, 2023) — our solver hands out the earliest-wrong-step label for free, so process supervision costs nothing.
  • Off the table at submission: tree-of-thought / MCTS / self-consistency — competition inference is greedy single-pass, so these were training-data tools only, never inference-time search.

7 · Scope, honesty & what's unexplored

What this is: a way to show and generate BIT (and the other types), and a generator substrate that made trying many custom CoT formats cheap and verifiable. What it isn't: a training recipe that teaches the model to solve gates, or a leaderboard claim.

Transformations — mostly unexplored, but the cipher rows are what shipped. We didn't push numeric CoT-format experimentation. The piece that did get used: the cipher (cryptarithm) transform rows from the same generator — about 2,500 verified rows (a CSP solve for the symbol→digit map plus honest priors) — were the one part of our own work that made it into our best scoring blend, on top of a strong public CoT base. Honest caveat: we couldn't cleanly attribute how much they added, and cipher-type accuracy stayed ~7.5%. The numeric lane remains the most open.

In hindsight, we likely needed more training. We earmarked about $150 — roughly $100 for Tinker (LoRA SFT / RL) and ~$50 / ~600 credits for Colab Pro+. We kept each experiment cheap — only about $2 of Tinker compute apiece — by running lots of small steps rather than a few big runs. Exact, free oracles mean every generated trace is usable without human labels or a learned verifier. Having CODEX (an agent) manage the Tinker runs end-to-end saved a lot of money — and bought serious volume: roughly 950 Tinker runs (≈500 training + ≈450 eval) over ~33 days, ~175 training-data builds, the BIT trace format revised through 30-plus numbered versions (up to V35), and 256 automated agent handoff notes.

Where the time went, and what our final submission actually was. Most of our effort went into trying to teach the model a different search method for BIT (the bisection / enumerate-test-prune work in §6). That bulk of the work never became a finished submission. We had hoped to transfer the search ability into the 30B; in the end our best end-to-end BIT result there was only about 12%. Our final leaderboard entry was small and almost incidental — we were just testing whether our cipher (cryptarithm) rows added any lift — and by the time we looked up, we'd run out of time.

If we went again, we'd prototype search-teaching on a small model first. The work became an exercise in trying to teach an LLM to search, and iterating that on a 30B is slow. Much of what seems to matter — trace shape, length budget, verifier-gated trimming, where the model fakes a step — looks largely model-agnostic, so we suspect those lessons would transfer up from a smaller model at a fraction of the cost per cycle. We can't prove it; it's the bet we'd make next time.

Why we're sharing work that hasn't paid off yet. It hasn't produced a meaningful result for us so far — ~12% end-to-end. Whether that was a failure on our part or something more fundamental, we honestly don't know. We suspect the missing piece may be mundane — not training long enough, the wrong-sized model, too small a budget. So we've included lots of training traces (the 5,891-row mini-skill dump and the CoT formats) to give others ideas to build on. Putting it in the open seems like the fastest way for someone to spot what we missed — that's the point of an open contribution.
